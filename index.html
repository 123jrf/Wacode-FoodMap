What is food insecurity and why does it matter?

In 2001, the United Nations agriculture organization formerly defined food insecurity. Food insecurity is defined as when an individual lacks regular access to enough safe and nutritious food for normal growth and development and an active and healthy life. The issue of food insecurity is a multifaceted problem that is faced by hundreds of millions around the world. Though the issue of food insecurity is linked to poverty, not all people living below the poverty line experience food insecurity and people living above the poverty line can experience food insecurity. Between 1990 and 2015 international food insecurity decreased by 17%, but has been rising again due to a number of factors. Currently 1 in 10 people worldwide are food insecure and the UN currently lists it as the number 2 global issue.

Who Does It Affect?

Food insecurity disproportionally affects Seniors, Children, Rural Communities, and Minorities and it varies among household types, but the rates within the following different groups are higher than the national average of 10.5 percent.

  • Households with children (14.8 percent).
  • Households with children headed by a single woman (27.7 percent) or a single man (16.3 percent).
  • Households with Black, non-Hispanic (21.7 percent) and Hispanic reference persons (17.2 percent; a household reference person is an adult household member in whose name the housing unit is owned or rented).
  • Households with incomes below 185 percent of the poverty threshold (28.6 percent; the Federal poverty line was $26,246 for a family of four in 2020).

What causes food insecurity

Many people in America struggle to meet their basic needs which increases their risk of food insecurity. Whether it’s an increase in the cost of living, unexpected costs, or lay-offs at work, a family may have to choose between buying food and paying bills.

The causes of food insecurity are complex. The following are some of the causes of food insecurity

  • Poverty, unemployment, or low income
  • Lack of affordable housing
  • Lack of access to public transportation
  • Chronic health conditions or lack of access to healthcare
  • Systemic racism and racial discrimination
